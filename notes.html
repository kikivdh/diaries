"To this end, art/archaeology is an invitation to use artefacts, traces, features, analyses
and perceptions from the past as if they were fresh, raw materials, palettes and tools in our
creation of original work that has political intention and impact. It is a search for new spaces
in which to make work that is both of the past, but alsowholly in the present. One way to conceive
of doing art/archaeology is through a three-step process: disarticulation, repurposing and disruption. "

From :Disarticulate—Repurpose—Disrupt: Art/Archaeology, by Doug Bailey


date: 15/01/21

"Art/Archaeology: the challenge

How is any of this archaeological, beyond the vague connection that is allowed at a subconscious level
of a past-in-the-present, or in addition to the thin recognition that objects that are disarticulated were
once of the past? Is the disarticulation-repurposingdisruption programme only applicable to contemporary art workers and work?


One answer is that none of it is very archaeological. Thus the work that takes place and its results and impacts occur in intellectual, disciplinary and institutional contexts beyond the boundaries of archaeology as traditionally practised. This non-archaeologicality is part of the proposal."


From :Disarticulate—Repurpose—Disrupt: Art/Archaeology, by Doug Bailey


date: 15/01/21

"The proposal, therefore, is to be politically affective, to locate the past honestly in the politics of the present, to make the world a better place in which to live. In many ways, archaeology does this already in declaring the inherently political character of the past, its recovery and its deployment in the present. The suggestion made here is for an additional, complementary venture: to disarticulate artefacts from their pasts and to release them into the contested dynamics of the present, through the making of new creative works, not traditionally seen as historic or archaeological in form, display or intention. Hence, the proposal for disruption within an art/archaeology.""

From :Disarticulate—Repurpose—Disrupt: Art/Archaeology, by Doug Bailey

“We can express our feelings regarding the world around us by poetic or by descriptive means. I prefer to express myself metaphorically. Let me stress: metaphorically, not symbolically. A symbol contains within itself a definite meaning, certain intellectual formula, while metaphor is an image. An image possessing the same distinguishing features as the world it represents. An image — as opposed to a symbol — is indefinite in meaning. One cannot speak of the infinite world by applying tools that are definite and finite. We can analyse the formula that constitutes a symbol, while metaphor is a being-within-itself, it's a monomial. It falls apart at any attempt of touching it.”

― Andrei Tarkovsky, 'Sculpting in Time'

date: 21/01/21

“Archive as Failed Futuristic Vision:

If Hirschhorn recovers radical figures in his archival work, Tacita Dean recalls lost souls in hers... Sebald surveys a modern world so devastated by history as to appear “after nature”: many of its inhabitants are “ghosts of repetition” (including the author) who seem at once “utterly liberated and deeply despondent.”37 These remnants are enigmatic, but they are enigmas without resolution, let alone redemption. Sebald even questions the humanist commonplace about the restorative power of memory; the ambiguous epigraph of the first section of The Emigrants reads: “and the last remnants memory destroys.”38 Dean also looks out on a forlorn world (often literally so in her films, videos, and photographs), yet for the most part she avoids the melancholic fixation that is the price that Sebald pays for his courageous refusal of redemptive illusion. The risk in her work is different: a romantic fascination with “human failing.”39 But, within the “failed futuristic visions” that she recovers archivally, there is also an intimation of the utopian—not as the other of reification (as in Hirschhorn) but as a concomitant of her archival presentation of the past as fundamentally heterogeneous and always incomplete.40”

― From 'An Archival Impulse', Hal Foster

date: 28/01/21

I think one of the primary characteristics of gifs is their repetition. Also, that they are low quality and thus very lightweight. Strange closed circuits.



There's something I can't let go of in these old(ish) 'memorial gifs', normally candles, wreaths, which burn or stay green forever on the screen. As opposed to a real candle, which invariably ends. Kind of wrapping up that state of mourning- a signifier of rememberance, during the time that the candle is lit. When the candle doesn't go out, you can't forget. This is a significant contract to me.

And it's this anti-amnesia gap which is difficult online. Having to remember everything at once is to remember nothing at all, or to be cowed by time...not sure. I just find them strange and sad, and I think the key is this repetition with no variation. Even grief's horseshoe is absent.


And actually I think that if pain (as a state) has value, it's all in the amnesia. How you can't quite ever remember the exact feeling and countours of the state- the impulse which allows women to give birth again, because they are allowed to forget the pain of the first time. I imagine it like a silvery fish going out of sight. To be in a state and forget it: to slough off pain, then remember it only in silhoutte... These gifs do a sort of innocent maintenance which is perhaps responsible, but never lets these states go. I think we are all entitled to some amnesiac spirit. I suppose losing political memory is dangerous, but so is retaining everything.
